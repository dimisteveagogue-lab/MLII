1. Classification vs Regression - Inference or Prediction? (2 points)

(1.1) We are interested in predicting the % change in the USD/Euro exchange rate in relation to the weekly changes in the world stock markets. Hence we collect weekly data for all of 2012. For each week we record the % change in the USD/Euro, the % change in the US market, the % change in the British market, and the % change in the German market.

  • Regression problem, because the response variable (% change in the USF/Euro exchange rate) is continuous.

  • Prediction, since the aim is to predict future value of the response based of provided features.

  • \(n = 52\)

  • \(p = 3 \:\text{predictors}\)

(1.2) A streaming service wants to understand which user behaviors are most strongly associated with cancellation of a subscription within the first 90 days. They examine the records of 12,000 recent sign-ups. For each user, they have recorded whether the user cancelled in the first 90 days, number of titles watched in the first 30 days, total minutes streamed in the first 30 days, payment plan, device type, and six other behavioral variables.

  • Classification problem, because the response variable (User cancelled in the first 90 days) is categorical.

  • Inference, since the main focus here is to identify strong associations between the response and the predictors

  • \(n = 12000\)

  • \(p = 10 \:\text{predictors}\)

(1.3) A regional power utility needs to forecast tomorrow’s peak electricity demand (in megawatts) so it can schedule generation capacity. They have a five-year history of daily peak demand. For each day they have recorded the peak demand, average temperature, average humidity, day of the week, and four other weather and calendar variables.

  • Regression problem, because the response variable (peak electricity demand) is continuous..

  • Prediction, since the goal is to determine tomorrow’s peak electricity demand based of prior observations for the past 5 years.

  • n = 1825

  • \(p = 7 \:\text{predictors}\)

(1.4) A bank wants to flag credit card transactions that are likely fraudulent at the moment they occur. They have a labeled history of 200,000 recent transactions, each annotated as fraudulent or legitimate. For each transaction they have recorded the amount, merchant category, time of day, distance from the cardholder’s billing address, and 16 other engineered features.

  • Classification problem, because the response variable (whether a transaction is fraudulent or not) is categorical.

  • Prediction, since the goal here is to predict whether a transaction is fraudulent at the time where it is occurring, based of 200,000 previously recorded transactions.

  • \(n = 200,000\)

  • \(p = 20 \:\text{predictors}\)

  • Two key advantages of a parametric approach in statistics/data science are:

    • Higher statistical power — When assumptions such as normality and equal variance are met, parametric tests extract more information from the data (using means, variances, and distributional structure), making them more sensitive and precise than non‑parametric methods.

    • More efficient estimation — Parametric models rely on a small set of parameters (e.g., mean and variance), allowing faster computation, clearer interpretation, and stronger inferential capabilities, especially with large datasets.

  • Two disadvantages of parametric approaches:

    • High bias : If strong formulated assumptions are not met, a parametric model results in highly biased predictions.

    • Under-fitting: Parametric approaches can be too simplistic or too rigid, which would lead to model performing poorly.

  • LLM’s critiques:

    • Critiques of the stated disadvantages

      High bias

      Your statement is directionally right, but it’s incomplete and slightly overstated.

      Critiques:

      • Bias only increases when assumptions are violated, not inherently because the model is parametric. Parametric models can be extremely low‑bias when the assumed form is correct.

      • “Highly biased predictions” is too vague. Bias depends on which assumption is violated (linearity, homoscedasticity, normality, independence), and the severity varies.

      • You should distinguish between model bias and estimator bias. Parametric models can have unbiased estimators even when the model itself is misspecified.

      • Not all assumption violations cause large bias. For example, mild non‑normality often barely affects regression estimates.

      A sharper version would specify which assumptions matter and how they distort estimates.

      Under‑fitting

      This is also correct in spirit, but it needs nuance.

      Critiques:

      • Under‑fitting is not inherent to parametric methods. It occurs only when the chosen parametric form is too simple relative to the true data‑generating process.

      • Parametric models can be extremely flexible. Generalized additive models, splines, and high‑order polynomials are still parametric but can fit complex patterns.

      • The issue is model selection, not parametric modeling itself. A poorly chosen parametric form underfits; a well‑chosen one does not.

      • You should contrast under‑fitting with over‑fitting. Parametric models can also overfit when too many parameters are used — the critique should acknowledge both sides.

      A more precise critique would emphasize that rigidity is a risk, not a guarantee.
